Losing a Language

How Belarusian disappeared from daily life but remained central to identity

By Alina Y. · Published June 28, 2026

Every 40 days, a language disappears somewhere in the world.

Belarusian has been in that danger zone for at least 16 years. UNESCO classifies it as a vulnerable language, meaning its long-term survival depends on whether new generations continue to learn and use it.

That designation may seem surprising. Belarusian is one of the country's two official state languages, alongside Russian. But official status does not necessarily guarantee everyday use.

We traced the language's decline in daily life, the pressures it faced in public space and culture, and the signs that it may still have a future.

14th–16th centuries

A Language Older Than the Belarusian State

Belarusian emerged from the East Slavic dialects spoken across the lands of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania. Between the 14th and 16th centuries, an early form of the language served as the language of administration, law and government.

This was not merely the language of folklore or local traditions. It was used by the chancery of one of Eastern Europe's largest states.

16th-20th centuries

Between Empires

The following centuries brought repeated pressure from more powerful state languages.

After the partitions of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth in 1772, 1793 and 1795, Belarusian lands became part of the Russian Empire, where Russian gradually displaced Belarusian from education, administration and publishing.

In interwar Poland, Belarusian schools and cultural organizations faced policies of Polonization. In the Soviet Union, a brief period of Belarusianization in the 1920s was followed by repression against national intellectuals and the growing dominance of Russian.

By the end of the 20th century, Belarusian remained an important marker of culture and identity, but had lost much of its presence in cities, education and public life.

1990s

A Brief Renaissance

The collapse of the Soviet Union seemed to offer a different future.

During the early 1990s, Belarusian experienced a revival. The language returned to schools, universities, media outlets and government institutions.

On March 15, 1994, the first Constitution of independent Belarus established Belarusian as the country's sole state language. For a brief moment, Belarusian became not only a language of culture and memory, but also the language of the state.

1994

Everything Changed in the Mid-1990s

In July 1994, Alexander Lukashenko was elected Belarus's first president. More than three decades later, he remains in power.

Less than a year after Lukashenko's election, Belarus held a national referendum. One of the questions asked voters whether Russian should receive equal status with Belarusian.

On May 14, 1995, Russian became a state language alongside Belarusian.

The decision marked a turning point in the country's language policy.

Over the following decades, Russian strengthened its position in cities, government institutions, media and everyday communication. Belarusian retained its official status, but its presence in public life gradually diminished.

A Native Language Spoken Less and Less

The consequences of those policy changes can be seen in Belarus's census data. For decades, Belarusians were asked two separate questions: what language they considered their native language, and what language they actually spoke at home. The gap between those answers tells the story of modern Belarusian better than almost any political speech.

In 1999, more than 7.4 million people said Belarusian was their native language. Ten years later, that number had fallen to just over 5 million. By 2019, it remained largely unchanged at about 5.1 million.

During the same period, the number of people who identified Russian as their native language grew from 2.4 million to nearly 4 million.

Yet the bigger shift appears not in identity, but in everyday life.

By 2019, more than 6.7 million people — about 71% of Belarus's population — reported speaking Russian at home.

In 1999, about 3.7 million Belarusians said they spoke Belarusian at home. By 2009, that figure had nearly halved, falling to 2.2 million.

The result is one of the central paradoxes of modern Belarus. Millions of people continue to consider Belarusian their native language. Far fewer use it in their daily lives.

How Belarusian Disappeared From Schools

Unlike the family, schools are shaped directly by state policy. As a result, changes in education often provide one of the clearest indicators of a language's future.

According to the Ministry of Education, roughly 40 percent of schools in Belarus still use Belarusian as the language of instruction. At first glance, that figure suggests the language remains widespread.

The reality is different.

Most Belarusian-language schools are small rural schools serving relatively few students. As a result, only about 10 percent of Belarusian schoolchildren receive their education in Belarusian.

The decline was not limited to education. It was also reflected in book publishing, newspapers and magazines — institutions that historically played a key role in keeping the language visible in public life.

The Internet Speaks Russian

If Belarusian was gradually disappearing from schools, books and newspapers, what happened to it online? To answer that question, we analyzed Google search behavior in Belarus over the past decade.

Using Google Trends, we compared 33 pairs of everyday search terms in Belarusian and Russian. The list included words people might use when looking for a job, an apartment, medical care, shopping information or daily news — terms such as "работа" / "праца" (job), "новости" / "навіны" (news), "квартира" / "кватэра" (apartment) and "врач" / "лекар" (doctor).

Throughout the entire period from 2015 to 2025, Russian-language searches overwhelmingly outnumbered their Belarusian equivalents.

In 2015, Russian forms appeared roughly 427 times more often than Belarusian ones. By 2023, that gap had widened to 853 to 1, the largest difference observed in the data. Even in 2025, Russian-language searches were still more than 580 times more common.

Culture Under Pressure

After the disputed 2020 presidential election, Belarus experienced the largest protests in its modern history. The authorities responded with mass arrests and a sweeping crackdown that extended beyond politics to independent media, civil society and the cultural sphere.

According to the Belarusian Council for Culture, the number of cultural figures sentenced to prison terms has increased almost every year since 2021.

In 2021, the organization recorded six cultural workers serving prison sentences. By 2024, that number had risen to 22. In 2025, it reached 31 — the highest figure in the monitoring period.

We also analyzed PEN Belarus data on books added to Belarus's official list of "extremist materials."

Of the 105 literary works included in the register, 77 were written in Belarusian. Only 28 were in Russian. The figures suggest that restrictions have disproportionately affected Belarusian-language literature.

A Different Story Emerges

At this point, the picture looks almost hopeless. But Google Trends data revealed another trend.

We compiled a second dataset focused not on everyday needs, but on Belarusian language, literature, history and national identity.

The list included searches for terms such as "беларуская мова" (Belarusian language), "тарашкевіца" (a traditional Belarusian orthography), writers including Yanka Kupala and Uladzimir Karatkevich, historical topics such as the Grand Duchy of Lithuania and the Belarusian People's Republic, and national symbols including the Pahonia coat of arms and the white-red-white flag.

Here, the pattern was entirely different.

From 2015 to 2019, interest remained relatively stable. Then, beginning in 2020, search activity started to rise sharply.

The trend peaked in 2022, when average interest in Belarusian language, culture and history was roughly 50 percent higher than in the late 2010s.

Methodology

This story combines historical sources with original data analysis. I used Belarusian census data, statistics from the Ministry of Education, monitoring data from the Belarusian Council for Culture, and PEN Belarus's register of books designated as “extremist materials”.

To analyze online language use, I collected Google Trends data for Belarus using the Pytrends library in Python. I compared 33 pairs of equivalent Belarusian- and Russian-language search queries from 2015 to 2025 and compiled a second dataset of searches related to Belarusian language, literature, history, and national identity. Google Trends data was aggregated into yearly averages using pandas.

To examine the language of books included in the extremist materials register, I parsed the bibliography with regular expressions (re) and classified titles using a custom rule-based language detector based on Belarusian- and Russian-specific orthographic and lexical features.

I also built a small interactive glossary in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript featuring Belarusian words that have no direct English equivalent. Each card uses a CSS flip animation, while the pronunciation is generated with the browser's Web Speech API.

Data cleaning and analysis were performed in Python using pandas, while visualizations were created in Flourish. The complete code and methodology are available on my GitHub.
